# Research Methods for JTBD

## Purpose

Practical guidance for gathering JTBD data through interviews, observation,
and proxy research. Choose the method that fits your situation and resources.

## Method Selection

| Method | Best For | Effort | Sample Size |
|---|---|---|---|
| Switch interviews | Understanding WHY people change solutions | Low (30-45 min each) | 8-12 |
| Outcome-driven interviews | Building comprehensive need set for a known job | Medium (60-90 min each) | 12-20 |
| Contextual inquiry | Discovering unknown jobs, understanding workarounds | High (2-4 hours each) | 6-10 |
| Proxy research | When no access to performers; hypothesis generation | Low | N/A |

Start with switch interviews if exploring. Use outcome-driven interviews when
committed to a specific job. Use contextual inquiry when the job is complex
and hard to articulate. Use proxy research as a last resort.

## Switch Interviews (Moesta)

Reverse-engineer the motivation behind a recent decision to change solutions.
Based on Moesta's four forces framework from "Demand-Side Sales 101."

### Who to Recruit

People who recently (within 90 days — memory fades fast):
- Started using your product
- Stopped using your product
- Evaluated your product and chose something else
- Switched from one solution to another in your category

### The Four Forces to Probe

**1. Push (frustration with current situation):**
"What was going wrong with what you had before?"
"Was there a specific moment when you thought 'I need something different'?"
"How long had the problem existed before you acted?"

**2. Pull (attraction to the new solution):**
"What caught your attention about [new solution]?"
"What outcome were you hoping for?"
"What did you imagine your life would be like after switching?"

**3. Anxiety (fear about the new):**
"What concerns did you have about switching?"
"What almost stopped you?"
"What would have happened if the new solution didn't work out?"

**4. Habit (inertia of the old):**
"What was actually good about your old approach?"
"What made you hesitate about leaving it?"
"What have you lost by switching?"

### Interview Flow

1. Set the scene (5 min): "Tell me about when you decided to [switch/buy/stop]"
2. Walk backward from the decision moment (15 min): timeline reconstruction
3. Probe each force (15 min): push, pull, anxiety, habit
4. Outcome check (5 min): "Did the new solution deliver what you hoped?"

### What to Listen For

- The trigger event that started the search (first thought)
- The hiring criteria that determined the choice
- Workarounds the new solution still doesn't solve
- Emotional language and energy shifts during the narrative

### The Buying Timeline

From Moesta's framework — six stages people move through:

1. **First Thought:** Creating space in the mind ("I'm not sleeping well")
2. **Passive Looking:** Noticing options that were invisible before
3. **Active Looking:** Deliberately comparing alternatives
4. **Deciding:** Making trade-offs and establishing value
5. **Onboarding:** First use — meeting or missing expectations
6. **Ongoing Use:** Building the habit (or not)

Events push people between stages. Map these events to understand causality.

## Outcome-Driven Interviews (Ulwick)

Systematically uncover desired outcomes at each stage of the job process.

### Who to Recruit

12-20 people who regularly perform the job. Include a mix:
- Heavy users (perform the job daily)
- Light users (perform occasionally)
- Struggling users (find the job difficult)

### Interview Flow

1. **Confirm the job** (10 min): Validate the core functional job statement
   with the performer. Adjust if their framing differs from yours.

2. **Walk the job map** (40-60 min): For each stage, ask:
   - "What are you trying to accomplish at this step?"
   - "How do you know this step is going well?"
   - "What could go wrong here?"
   - "What would perfect look like?"

3. **Translate to outcomes**: Convert responses to desired outcome format.
   "I want to make sure nothing falls through the cracks" becomes
   "Minimize the likelihood that a task is overlooked during handoff."

4. **Emotional/social jobs** (10 min): "How do you feel when this goes
   well? Badly? Who notices?"

5. **Circumstances** (5 min): "Under what conditions does this job become
   significantly harder?"

### Output Target

- Thorough analysis: 50-150 outcome statements across all stages
- Lightweight pass: 15-30 outcome statements focused on highest-friction stages
- Diminishing returns typically around 12 interviews

## Contextual Inquiry

Observe people performing the job in their real environment.

### What to Watch For

- **Workarounds:** Sticky notes, side spreadsheets, duplicate entry — each
  signals an unmet need
- **Tool switching:** How many apps/windows touched? Each switch is friction.
- **Things they skip:** Steps bypassed or quality checks ignored
- **Emotional signals:** Sighing, redoing work, frustration, visible pride

### Questions During Observation

- "What are you doing right now? Why?"
- "Is this typical or unusual today?"
- "What happens if you skip this step?"
- "Show me the hardest part."

## When You Can't Do Primary Research

Use proxy methods, but always label output as "hypothesized — needs validation."

1. **Mine support tickets and NPS comments** for job-related language
2. **Interview customer-facing teams** (support, sales, CS) about repeated frustrations
3. **Analyze competitor reviews** on G2, Capterra, app stores — negative
   reviews reveal unmet needs, positive reviews reveal jobs being satisfied
4. **Run a lightweight survey:** "Describe the last time you tried to [job].
   What was hardest?"
5. **Search forums, Reddit, communities** where your performers discuss
   their struggles

### Sample Size Guidelines

| Goal | Minimum | Ideal |
|---|---|---|
| Exploratory | 6-8 | 12-15 |
| Validate a specific job | 8-12 | 15-20 |
| Full outcome set | 12-15 | 20-30 |
| Quantitative survey (after qualitative) | 100+ | 200-600 |
