## The Four Forces of Progress

Every switch decision is governed by four forces. People change only when the driving forces overcome the resisting forces: (Push + Pull) > (Anxiety + Habit).

### Force 1: Push of the current situation (drives change)

The frustration, struggle, or dissatisfaction with how things work today. Without a push, there's no demand.

**Examples:**
- "I was tossing and turning every night and it was affecting my work" (mattress)
- "I was spending 3 hours every week manually assembling status reports" (project tool)
- "I knew I had to change my career but couldn't figure out how" (education)

**Interview questions:**
- "What was going wrong that made you start looking for something new?"
- "Can you describe the specific moment when you thought 'I need to change this'?"
- "How long had you been living with this problem before you acted?"

### Force 2: Pull of the new solution (drives change)

The magnetism of a better future — the imagined progress. This is the "new me" the performer envisions.

**Examples:**
- "I imagined waking up refreshed and being productive all day" (mattress)
- "I saw a demo and thought — we could cut our standup meetings in half" (project tool)
- "I pictured myself in a career I was proud of, providing for my family" (education)

**Interview questions:**
- "When you first saw [the product/solution], what did you imagine it would be like?"
- "What did you hope would be different about your life after making the change?"
- "Who or what inspired you to think a better way was possible?"

### Force 3: Anxiety of the new solution (blocks change)

Fear, uncertainty, and risk associated with switching. Often the most underestimated force — and the most powerful lever for unlocking demand.

**Examples:**
- "What if I spend $1,000 and the new mattress is just as bad?" (mattress)
- "What if the team refuses to adopt it and we've wasted months migrating?" (project tool)
- "What if I can't handle the coursework and I've wasted money and time?" (education)

**Interview questions:**
- "What almost stopped you from making the switch?"
- "What were your biggest concerns or fears about the new solution?"
- "What would have to be true for you to feel safe trying this?"

**Key insight:** Reducing anxiety often creates more demand than adding features. Casper's 100-day return policy, SNHU's simplified application, free trials with no credit card — these are all anxiety reducers, not product improvements.

### Force 4: Habit of the present (blocks change)

The comfort, familiarity, and sunk cost of the current way. Even bad solutions have inertia.

**Examples:**
- "My old mattress was terrible, but at least I knew what to expect" (mattress)
- "We'd already invested months customizing our spreadsheet workflow" (project tool)
- "I'd been out of school for 15 years — I wasn't sure I could be a student again" (education)

**Interview questions:**
- "What was good enough about what you had before?"
- "What made you hesitate or delay?"
- "What investment had you already made in the current approach that was hard to walk away from?"

## The Buying Timeline

Buying unfolds over a predictable sequence. Understanding where customers are on this timeline helps you intervene at the right moment.

### Stage 1: First Thought
The first time the performer consciously recognizes the struggle. "I'm not sleeping well." "This is a dead-end job." Before this thought, there's no mental space to file information about solutions. After it, they start noticing things they didn't notice before.

**Implication:** Create first thoughts through questions, stories, new metrics, or stating the obvious — not feature announcements.

### Stage 2: Passive Looking
The performer notices options as they go through life, but isn't actively searching. They drive past the mattress store and now notice it. They hear a friend mention a tool. This can last months or years if no event pushes them forward.

**Implication:** Be present where performers passively browse. Content, word-of-mouth, ambient visibility matter here.

### Stage 3: Active Looking
A triggering event moves them from passive to active. They invest time, effort, and money to research options. They bring others into the process. They need options as reference points to build an ideal solution in their mind. They diverge before they converge.

**Implication:** Provide comparison frameworks, not just product information. Help them evaluate, not just discover.

### Stage 4: Deciding
They make trade-offs and commit. A "time wall" — real or artificial — forces the decision. Without urgency, people look forever.

**Implication:** Understand the trade-offs your customers make. Every purchase involves sacrifice — what are they willing to give up?

### Stage 5: Onboarding
The first experience with the new solution. Expectations from the buying process collide with reality. This is where satisfaction or regret is formed.

**Implication:** Design onboarding to deliver early progress. If the customer doesn't feel progress quickly, the forces of habit pull them back.

### Stage 6: Ongoing Use
The new solution becomes the new habit — or doesn't. The customer either builds new routines around it or reverts.

**Implication:** Continuous progress matters more than final outcomes. Customers evaluate whether things are getting better at every touchpoint.

## Mapping Forces to Timeline

| Timeline Stage | Dominant Forces | What to Investigate |
|---------------|----------------|-------------------|
| First Thought | Push | What struggling moment triggers awareness? |
| Passive Looking | Push + early Pull | What information do they encounter? Who influences them? |
| Active Looking | Pull + Anxiety | What options do they compare? What concerns emerge? |
| Deciding | All four in tension | What trade-offs do they make? What creates urgency? |
| Onboarding | Anxiety + Habit | What friction blocks early progress? What causes abandonment? |
| Ongoing Use | Habit (new) | How does the product become part of their routine? |
