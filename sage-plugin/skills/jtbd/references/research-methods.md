## Method Selection

| Method | Best For | Effort |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Switch interviews | Understanding why people change solutions | Low (30–45 min, 8–12 interviews) |
| Outcome-driven interviews | Building a comprehensive need set for a known job | Medium (60–90 min, 12–20 interviews) |
| Contextual inquiry | Discovering unknown jobs; understanding workarounds | High (2–4 hours, 6–10 sessions) |

Start with switch interviews if exploring. Use outcome-driven interviews when committed to a job. Use contextual inquiry when the job is complex and hard to articulate.

## Switch Interviews (Moesta)

Reverse-engineer the motivation behind a recent decision to change solutions.

**Recruit:** People who recently started using your product, stopped using it, or evaluated it and chose something else. Within last 90 days — memory fades fast.

**Probe the four forces:**

1. **Push** (frustration with current solution): "What was going wrong? What was the specific moment you thought 'I need something else'?"
2. **Pull** (attraction to alternative): "What caught your attention? What outcome were you hoping for?"
3. **Anxiety** (fear about switching): "What concerns did you have? What almost stopped you?"
4. **Habit** (inertia): "What was good about what you had before? What made you hesitate?"

**Flow:** Set the scene (5 min) → Walk backward from adoption moment (15 min) → Probe each force (15 min) → Outcome check (5 min).

**Listen for:** The trigger event that started the search. The hiring criteria. Workarounds the new solution still doesn't solve. Emotional signals. **Compensatory behaviors** — when people use products in unintended ways (e.g., using baking soda as a cleaner, Segways for police patrols) — these signal unmet jobs.

**Probe the four forces during every switch interview.** For each force, ask about functional, emotional, and social dimensions. See [forces-and-timeline.md](forces-and-timeline.md) for the complete force-by-force question set.

## Outcome-Driven Interviews (Ulwick)

Systematically uncover desired outcomes at each stage of the job process.

**Recruit:** 12–20 people who regularly perform the job. Mix heavy users, light users, and people who struggle.

**Flow:**
1. **Confirm the job** (10 min): Validate the core functional job statement.
2. **Walk the job map** (40–60 min): For each stage, ask:
   - "What are you trying to accomplish here?"
   - "How do you know this stage is going well?"
   - "What could go wrong?"
   - "What would perfect look like?"
3. **Translate to outcomes**: Convert responses to desired outcome format (direction + measure + object + clarifier). "I want to make sure nothing falls through the cracks" → "Minimize the likelihood that a task is overlooked during handoff."
4. **Emotional/social jobs** (10 min): "How do you feel when this goes well? Badly? Who notices?"
5. **Circumstances** (5 min): "Under what conditions does this job become significantly harder?"

**Aim for:** 50–150 outcomes across all stages for thorough analysis; 15–30 for a lightweight pass.

## Contextual Inquiry

Observe people performing the job in their real environment.

**Watch for:**
- **Workarounds:** Sticky notes, side spreadsheets, duplicate entry — each signals an unmet need
- **Tool switching:** How many apps/windows touched to complete the job? Each switch is friction.
- **Things they skip:** Steps bypassed, quality checks they don't bother with
- **Emotional signals:** Sighing, redoing work, expressed frustration, visible pride

**Ask during observation:** "What are you doing now? Why?" "Is this typical or unusual today?" "What happens if you skip this step?"

Take photos (with permission) of workspace, screens, artifacts.

## Sample Sizes

| Goal | Minimum | Ideal |
|------|---------|-------|
| Exploratory | 6–8 | 12–15 |
| Validate a specific job | 8–12 | 15–20 |
| Full outcome set | 12–15 | 20–30 |
| Quantitative survey (after qual) | 100+ | 200–600 |

Diminishing returns typically around 12 interviews.

**Recruiting tips:** Get actual performers, not proxies. Include a range of proficiency levels. Avoid recruiting only enthusiasts.

## When You Can't Do Primary Research

1. Mine support tickets and NPS comments for job-related language
2. Interview customer-facing teams (support, sales, CS) about repeated frustrations
3. Analyze competitor reviews on G2, Capterra, or app stores — negative reviews reveal unmet needs
4. Run a lightweight survey: "Describe the last time you tried to [job]. What was hardest?"

Label the output "hypothesized — needs validation."
